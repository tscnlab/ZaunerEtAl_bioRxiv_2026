1 Introduction

1.1 Short intro + problem statement

Human health and ocular light exposure are intricately linked through non-visual neural pathways from the eye into the autonomous nervous system. Because light exposure at the eye level is not only shaped by the solar potential, but also by geographic location, culture, the built environment, and, ultimately, individual behaviour, it is considered a highly modifiable determinant of human health. However, to date, we have a limited understanding about personal light exposure patterns under naturalistic conditions, how much it varies within and between populations, and how it is determined by individual, behavioural, and environmental conditions. In this study, we provide the first multi-country wearable and experience-sampling dataset in terms of measurement fidelity and scope, and decompose personal light exposure data into contributing components.

1.2 circadian rhythms and light

Human behaviour and physiology is shaped by the 24-hour day and night cycle. The evolutionary benefit of being able to anticipate changes in the environment and act upon them, instead of merely reacting, has led to the development of an endogenous circadian rhythm. In humans, as in many other mammals, the circadian system is conducted by a central pacemaker, the Suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN), situated in the hypothalamus of the brain. The SCN regulates many physiological processes in a major way, either directly, or indirectly by setting the peripheral clocks within all cells of the body. Circadian effects include hormone production, such as Melatonin and Cortisol, sleep-wake regulation, as well as mood, cognitive, and physical performance. In an entrained, i.e., aligned system, internal processes provide the ideal substrate to handle exterior stressors during the day and interior stressors at night. A property of the SCN is its flexibility of alignment, that allows readjustments of the endogenous rhythm through external stimuli, so-called Zeitgeber. The strongest Zeitgeber for humans is light input through the eyes. Intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells (ipRGCs) in the retina relay environmental light levels through the retino-hypothalamic tract directly to the SCN. Secondary downstream targets of the ipRGCs are sleep-wake regulating centers of the brain, and possibly also mood-regulating centers as found in the mouse model. This link between retinal light exposure and acute and longterm effects on human physiology has wide-reaching implications for human health and wellbeing, as a misaligned circadian system increases the risk for mental, metabolic, cardiovascular disease, as well as for breast and prostate cancer.

1.3 laboratory research in the past decades

Controlled laboratory studies over the past decades have allowed to characterize the novel receptor type in the human retina, as well as describe the downstream effects on physiology. In short, light that excites the ipRGCs acutely increases alertness, suppresses melatonin synthetization in the pineal gland, and phase-shifts the circadian rhythm, collectively summarized as non-visual effects of light. Through the photopigment Melanopsin, ipRGCs are maximally sensitive around 490 nm, i.e., shorter wavelengths compared to daytime brightness perception (~555 nm). ipRGC are further sparse in number (about 1% of all RGCs, though widely distributed), sluggish in their reaction, and integrating in their behavior. This makes them ideal daylight detectors, however, they can be excited by any type of lightsource. The spectral sensitivity of the ipRCG has been standardized by the CIE in the standard CIE S 026/2018, with the melanopic equivalent daylight illuminance (melanopic EDI) as the primary measure to describe a corneal stimulus to the non-visual system. It has since been shown that melanopic EDI are superior to describe non-visual effects in a dose-effect-dependent manner compared to all other single photoreceptor sensitivities or indeed photopic illuminance, i.e., daytime brightness perception. Based on this, recommendations for healthy light exposure have been published that are based on the excitability of the non-visual system, with a minimum of 250 lx melanopic EDI during waking hours to support circadian synchronisation and alertness, and a maximum of 10 lx melanopic EDI during the three hours before sleep to support a shift towards rest. The sleeping environment should be as dark as possible, with an upper threshold of 1 lx melanopic EDI if light cannot be avoided.

1.4 Real-world findings

While laboratory studies brought insights into the mechanisms, longterm effects of light exposure on human health require observational studies under naturalistic conditions. 4.1 Research questions and hypotheses

We assessed the following research questions and hypotheses as specified in the preregistration and Section 4.11:

Research Question 1:

Do sites differ in objectively measured personal light exposure? If so, does an individual’s behaviour and micro-environment have a stronger effect than site?

H1: There is a significant difference in personal light-exposure metrics across sites, even after accounting for latitude and photoperiod.

H2: The variance of personal light exposure patterns (hourly geometric mean of melanopic EDI) by participants within sites is greater than the variance between sites.

4.11 Deviation from the preregistration

This section summarizes deviations from the preregistration (https://aspredicted.org/te3zw2.pdf)

4.11.1 Preprocessing

  • During preprocessing, only non-wear periods that did not occur within a sleep window were set to NA.
  • For sleep-environment-related analyses, sleep windows were defined based on sleepprep, that is, the time at which participants reported trying to sleep, rather than the estimated sleep onset time (sleepprep + sleepdelay). This distinction primarily affects analyses related to the Brown recommendations for healthy light exposure during wake, pre-sleep, and sleep periods. This change aligns the requirements for the sleep environment with the intention to sleep.
  • Metric update: the geometric mean of melanopic EDI during the 10 darkest hours of the day (lx) was used instead of the 5 darkest hours. This approach is consistent with the midpoint of the darkest 10 hours of the day and provides a more stable time frame. Regularly a participant-night would contain more than 5 hours of darkness, making the choice of which 5-hour period is chosen somewhat arbitrary. Using a longer timeframe avoids this effectively.
  • Metric update: the midpoint of the longest period above 250 lx melanopic EDI was replaced by the mean timing of exposure above 250 lx melanopic EDI, because the latter does not reduce the day to a single event.

4.11.2 R1

  • In H1, latitude had to be accounted for separately from site. Including both terms in the same model is problematic because each site is associated with a specific latitude, which effectively removes the estimable effect of latitude. Therefore, model comparison based on AIC was used to assess which model provided the better fit.
  • In H2, an sz basis was used to model the smooth interaction between time and site instead of a cyclic spline. Although this approach does not force the estimates and derivatives at the boundaries of the spline, that is, midnight to midnight, to be identical, the sz basis addresses identifiability issues in these models that would otherwise persist.
  • In H2, a main effect of time was added so that the site-specific smooths model only deviations from the overall temporal pattern.
  • In H2, average melanopic EDI per 30-minute interval was used instead of the hourly geometric mean. This was because model diagnostics indicated that the model captured the average on a logarithmic scale substantially better than the geometric mean. To reduce potential bias introduced by arithmetic averaging, the aggregation window was shortened from 1 hour to 30 minutes.

4.11.3 R2

  • In H3 and H4, site was included as a fixed effect rather than a random effect to improve the stability of model fitting.
  • In H3 and H4, generalized linear mixed-effects models with a Tweedie error distribution were used because they provided better model performance.
  • In H6, the hourly geometric mean was used instead of derived exposure metrics. This decision was made because the large number of candidate metrics, combined with the number of predictors, did not yield a parsimonious or interpretable model structure. The revised hypothesis is: There is a significant relationship between day type, daily exercise, sleep, and the hourly geometric mean of light exposure.
  • In H7, only parameter combinations that were significant in H2 were retained. In addition, an interaction model was not used because the joint parameter space of latitude and photoperiod was sparse. Therefore, only photoperiod was included, and the hypothesis was revised to: There is a ceiling effect of photoperiod on level-, duration-, and exposure-history-based metrics. H7 was moved to R1 because it fits better there thematically. The original numbering was retained to facilitate cross-referencing with the preregistration.
